Chapter 08

So What
Do You
Do About It?

You've made it through the full story of media and politics. Now the question is: what does that mean for how you consume information?

Why You Should Care

Democracy runs on informed citizens. If the information we consume is distorted — by bias, misinformation, echo chambers, or algorithm-driven outrage — then our political decisions are built on a shaky foundation. Being a savvy media consumer isn't just a personal skill. It's a civic responsibility. The health of democracy is connected to the quality of information we demand, share, and believe.

Reflect on Your Own Media Habits

Have you ever shared something online without checking if it was true first?

You're not alone. Research shows the vast majority of people — including highly educated ones — have shared content they didn't fully verify. The speed of social media is designed to make you react before you think. Here's how to slow down: before sharing anything political, ask yourself three questions: Where did this come from? Can I verify this with a second source? Am I sharing because it's true or because it confirms what I already believe?
That's genuinely rare — and admirable. Most people have shared something questionable at some point. Keep that critical habit going. One good practice: before sharing political content, spend 60 seconds checking if a credible news outlet has also reported it. If you can't find it elsewhere, that's a red flag.

Do you think your social media feed shows you a politically balanced view of the world?

It's worth testing this assumption. Algorithms are very good at creating the illusion of balance while actually narrowing your information diet. Try this experiment: search for a major political topic you care about, but look specifically for the best version of the opposing argument. You may be surprised how rarely it appears in your normal feed — and how different the issue looks from that angle.
Good self-awareness. Knowing your feed is biased is the first step. The next step is actively seeking out credible sources that challenge your existing views — not to change your mind necessarily, but to understand why people think differently. The goal isn't agreement; it's a more accurate picture of reality.

Do you trust most of the news you see?

Some trust is healthy — but healthy skepticism is better. Not all news is equally reliable. Mainstream outlets like AP, Reuters, and PBS NewsHour have strong track records of corrections and sourcing. Opinion shows, fringe websites, and social media posts require much more skepticism. The key is not to trust or distrust all media equally — it's to evaluate sources case by case.
Healthy skepticism is good — but total distrust is its own trap. When we distrust everything, we become vulnerable to those who exploit that cynicism — often saying "the mainstream media is lying, only I'm telling you the truth." Complete media distrust can actually make you easier to manipulate, not harder. The goal is critical evaluation, not blanket rejection.
How to Be a Smarter Media Consumer
01

Check Your Sources

Not all news sources are equal. Learn which outlets have strong editorial standards and track records of accuracy. Use fact-checking sites like PolitiFact, FactCheck.org, and Snopes to verify claims before believing or sharing them.

02

Diversify Your Feed

Actively seek out credible sources that cover politics from different angles. You don't have to agree with them — but understanding how others see the world makes you more informed and less susceptible to manipulation.

03

Notice Emotional Triggers

If a headline makes you immediately furious or triumphant — pause. That reaction is exactly what clickbait and misinformation are designed to create. Strong emotions are a signal to slow down and verify, not to share immediately.

04

Separate News from Opinion

Learn to distinguish news reporting (what happened) from opinion and commentary (what someone thinks about it). Both have value, but they're different things. Most of primetime cable news and political podcasting is opinion, not news.

05

Avoid Bias — In Yourself

We all have confirmation bias — the tendency to believe information that confirms our existing beliefs. The best antidote is actively reading the strongest version of arguments you disagree with, from credible sources that make them.

06

Think Critically — Always

Ask: Who created this? Why? What do they want me to believe or feel? Whose perspective is missing? What evidence is provided? How would I know if this was wrong? Critical thinking is the most important media literacy skill there is.
